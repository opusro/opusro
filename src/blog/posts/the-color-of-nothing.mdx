We spent three weeks choosing the background color of our website. Not because we're precious about hex codes — but because `#050505` is not `#000000`, and the difference matters more than you'd think.

## Pure Black Is a Lie

True black — `#000000` — doesn't exist in nature. Even the darkest night sky registers somewhere around `#0a0a0f` once your eyes adjust. Vantablack, the famous carbon nanotube coating, absorbs 99.965% of light. Not 100%. Nothing absorbs 100%.

When you put pure black behind white text on a screen, something subtle happens: the contrast is so extreme that your eyes fatigue. The text develops a kind of halo. Reading becomes slightly, almost imperceptibly harder. You don't notice it on a single paragraph. You notice it on the tenth.

```
#000000 — Pure black    — 100% contrast — aggressive
#050505 — Near black    — 97% contrast  — grounded
#0a0a0a — Warm black    — 94% contrast  — receding
#111111 — Soft black    — 89% contrast  — atmospheric
```

We landed on `#050505`. It's dark enough to feel black. It's warm enough to feel *real*.

## The Grey Scale of Function

Our entire blog uses exactly four greys. Not because we couldn't use more — but because each one has a *job*:

  caption="Constraints breed intention."
/>

| Color | Hex | Role |
|-------|---------|------|
| Primary text | `#ffffff` | Headlines, body copy, anything the reader *must* see |
| Secondary text | `#999999` | Excerpts, subtitles, supporting information |
| Tertiary text | `#666666` | Dates, metadata, captions — information you *can* see |
| Borders | `#222222` | Structure, separation, invisible scaffolding |

That's it. Four values. Every grey on this page comes from that list.

### Why Not More?

Because every additional shade is a decision the reader's brain has to process. *Is this slightly lighter grey more important or less important than that other slightly lighter grey?* The cognitive overhead is invisible but cumulative.

With four levels, the hierarchy is instant:
- **White** = read this
- **Light grey** = read this if you want context
- **Dark grey** = glance at this for orientation
- **Darkest grey** = you'll never consciously see this, but you'd miss it if it were gone

## The One Exception

There is exactly one place where we use a color that isn't greyscale: error states. A failed form submission turns `#ff6b6b` — a muted red that says *something went wrong* without screaming.

We considered keeping errors in grey too. We tested it. Users didn't notice their submission had failed. So: one red, for one purpose. The exception that proves the rule.

> Color is information. Use it only when you have information to convey.

## Typography as Color

Here's something most people don't realize about black-and-white design: **font weight is your color palette**.

- `font-weight: 800` on a headline *feels* white-hot. Loud. Present.
- `font-weight: 400` on body text *feels* cool. Calm. Steady.
- `font-weight: 300` on a subtitle *feels* grey — even when the hex value is `#ffffff`.

We use weight the way most sites use color: to create emphasis, hierarchy, and emotion. An 800-weight title next to a 300-weight subtitle creates the same visual tension as a red button on a blue page — but without any of the noise.
