The attention economy has failed us. Every app fights for your time, your focus, your *presence*. Notifications cascade. Feeds refresh. The loop never ends.

We decided to build differently.

## The Problem

Most software is designed around a single metric: engagement. More time in-app means more ad impressions, more data collected, more revenue extracted. The user is not the customer — they are the product.

This is not a new observation. But knowing the problem and building the alternative are very different things.

> The best tool is the one you forget you're using. It does its job and gets out of the way.

When we started OPUS, we asked a simple question: **what if software respected the human on the other side of the screen?**

## What We Mean by Human Experience Design

Human Experience Design is not a rebrand of UX. It's a constraint system. Every feature we ship must pass a simple test:

- Does this **give** time back to the user, or **take** it?
- Does this create calm, or anxiety?
- Would we use this ourselves at 11pm, exhausted, just wanting a moment of peace?

If the answer to any of these is wrong, we don't ship it.

## Opus Loop as a Case Study

Opus Loop is a meditation and soundscape app. There are hundreds of these. Most of them want you to subscribe, browse a library of thousands of sessions, track streaks, share achievements, and come back every day.

We went the opposite direction:

1. **No subscription.** Pay once, own it forever.
2. **No content library.** You bring your own soundscapes or use the built-in ones.
3. **No streaks.** We don't want you to feel guilty for missing a day.
4. **No social features.** Your mindful moments are yours.

The result is an app that does less — and because of that, does *more* for the person using it.

## Building What Matters

We're a small studio in Romania. We don't have the resources to compete with venture-backed companies on feature count. And we don't want to.

Our competitive advantage is restraint.

Every line of code we write, every pixel we place, every interaction we design — we ask whether it serves the human or the metric. When those two conflict, the human wins.
